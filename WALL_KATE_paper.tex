\begin{abstract}
We present a preconditioner for solving fractional differential equations (FDEs) on an adaptive mesh. Adaptive refinement of the problem domain results in a stiffness matrix with Toeplitz blocks along the main diagonal, while the FDE yields a dense stiffness matrix, where off-diagonal blocks are stored as low-rank approximations. Our adaptive preconditioner utilizes ideas from the circulant preconditioner of Chan and Strang [SIAM Journal on Scientific Computing, 1989], which takes advantage of the Toeplitz blocks on the diagonal and accounts for the low-rank nature of the off-diagonal blocks. We demonstrate its effectiveness at accelerating convergence for our systems and emphasize its efficient application. This work presents theoretical results about the spectral clustering of the preconditioned system. In order to prove these results, special consideration is taken on how the low-rank blocks perturb the eigenvalues of the Toeplitz block-diagonal system. Numerical tests are used to inspect any assumptions and validate our results.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} 
% Availabilitty of Xiaozhe's code / stiffness matrices 
In this paper we consider fractional differential equations \\ (FDEs) of the form 
\begin{equation}\label{FDE}
	D^{\alpha}_x u(x) = f(x), \ x \in (a,b),
\end{equation}
where $D^{\alpha}_x$ is the Riesz fractional derivative of order $1 < \alpha < 2$ and $f \in L^2([a,b])$. 

Many numerical solvers for FDEs discretize the domain on a uniform mesh; the uniform mesh results in a linear system with a Toeplitz matrix. Still FDEs present unique difficulties in numerical methods due to the non-locality of the operator resulting in a dense stiffness matrix. Additionally, as part of the fractional derivative the singular kernel, $k(x,s) = |x-s|^{1-\alpha}$, is integrated, resulting in end-point singularities which make Galerkin finite-element methods sub-optimal \cite{hierarch,superfast}. To address this, Jin and Zhou developed a reconstruction technique that isolates the singularity to improve FEM convergence \cite{JZ}. Alternatively, Wang and Du developed a Toeplitz preconditioner that reduces iteration cost to $\bigOh(n \log n)$ using the preconditioned conjugate gradient squared method \cite{superfast}. 

The above methods, however, require uniform meshes. An optimal mesh would have the $L^{\infty}$ error equally distributed on each interval, but a uniform mesh does not in general satisfy this condition. Indeed, the singularities of FDEs force higher error in the intervals near the boundary. Alternatively, to achieve desired accuracy across the whole domain we use use an adaptive mesh and adaptive finite-element method (AFEM) \cite{AFEM, Xiaozhe}. The usual AFEM algorithm iterates through the process 
\begin{equation} \label{AFEM}
	\texttt{SOLVE} \rightarrow \texttt{ESTIMATE} \rightarrow \texttt{MARK} \rightarrow \texttt{REFINE},
\end{equation} 
but the computational cost is dominated by the \texttt{SOLVE} step \cite{Xiaozhe}. 

Our aim is to build a preconditioner for the stiffness matrix, $\A$, from an AFEM discretization of Equation (\ref{FDE}) that will reduce this work. We investigate what structure the adaptive mesh retains and how that is reflected in the structure of $\A$. Then, using preconditioning techniques formulated for Toeplitz matrices \cite{Chan_89, Chan_Strang, Chan_Yeung, schrod}, we build a preconditioner, show the preconditioned system has eigenvalues clustered around 1 and demonstrate numerical results with superlinear convergence.

In the usual finite-element method (FEM) setting an uniform mesh results in a Toeplitz stiffness matrix. In the adaptive setting, after an initial solve on a uniform grid, the error on each element is estimated and the elements with largest error are refined via bisection.

\section{Background}
\subsection{Properties of Structured Matrices}
There has long been interest in solving Toeplitz linear systems efficiently. A matrix $A$ is called Toeplitz if $a_{ij} = a_{i-j}$. Arbitrary $n \times n$ matrices have up to $n^2$ entries and are, in general, solved directly in $\bigOh(n^3)$ time. Since a Toeplitz matrix has at most $2n-1$ unique entries, we expect to be able to solve it in $\bigOh (n^2)$ time, see for example Levinson's algorithm \cite{Levinson}. Even this improvement, however, is infeasible for large systems. Instead we turn to iterative Krylov and multigrid methods \cite{schrod, hierarch, Xiaozhe}. For these methods we take advantage of Toeplitz structure by using circulant preconditioners. 

Toeplitz matrices commonly arise in partial differential equations discretization, FDEs, signal processing, and control theory. Often the Toeplitz matrices are also symmetric positive definite (SPD). Given an SPD Toeplitz system $\Toe x = b$, the idea introduced by Strang and Chan \cite{Chan_Strang} is to use certain circulant preconditioners $C$ so that $C^{-1}Tx = C^{-1}b$ is solved in fewer iterations.
A circulant matrix is a Toeplitz matrix, that additionally has the ``wrap-around'' property where the last entry of each row is the first entry of the subsequent row:
\begin{equ}[H]
	\begin{equation}
		\Toe = \begin{pmatrix}
			\toe_0  &  \toe_{-1} & \cdots & \toe_{-(n-1)} \\
			\toe_{1} & \toe_0 & \cdots & \toe_{-(n-2)} \\
			\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
			\toe_{n-1} & \toe_{n-2} & \cdots & \toe_0 
		\end{pmatrix} 
		\hspace{.15 in}
		C = \begin{pmatrix}
			c_0 & c_1 & \cdots & c_{n-1} \\
			c_{n-1} & c_0 & \cdots & c_{n-2} \\
			\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
			c_{1} & c_{2} & \cdots & c_0 
		\end{pmatrix}
	\end{equation}
	\caption{$\Toe$ is a Toeplitz matrix and $C$ is circulant.}
\end{equ}

As discussed in Section (\ref{stiffness}), the stiffness matrix resulting from AFEM applied to Equation (\ref{FDE}) is not Toeplitz, but does contain SPD Toeplitz blocks. 
Toeplitz matrices have many special properties, but we focus on their connection to functions in the Wiener class. 
\begin{definition}
	Given a singly infinite, symmetric Toeplitz matrix $\Toe$ we define the finite subsection $\Toe_m$ to be the $m \times m$ principle submatrix. That is, 
	\begin{equation*}
		\Toe = \begin{pmatrix}
			\toe_0 & \toe_1 & \toe_2 & \\
			\toe_1 & \toe_0 & \toe_1 &  \ddots \\
			\toe_2 & \toe_1 & \toe_0 & \ddots  \\
			& \ddots & \ddots & \ddots  \\
		\end{pmatrix}
				\Toe_m = \begin{pmatrix}
			\toe_0 & \toe_1 & \cdots & \toe_{m-1} \\
			\toe_1 & \toe_0 & \cdots & \toe_{m-2} \\
			\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
			\toe_{m-1} & \toe_{m-2} & \cdots & \toe_0 
		\end{pmatrix}.
	\end{equation*}
	Assume $\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} |\toe_k| <\infty$, then the matrices induce functions $\toe(z) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \toe_k z^k$ and $\toe_m(z) = \sum_{k=-(m-1)}^{m-1} \toe_k z^k$ are real, positive, and in the Wiener class for $|z| = 1$.
\end{definition}

Previous work on Toeplitz systems offers a few options for circulant preconditioners. Although their construction differs, the spectrum of the preconditioned systems are asymptotically the same \cite{Chan_89}. Following \cite{Bini}, given a symmetric Toeplitz matrix $\Toe$ we build a Hankel correction, $H$, where 
\begin{equation} \label{Hank}
	\Toe =

		\toe_1 & \toe_0 & \toe_1 &  \cdots &\toe_{n-3}& \toe_{n-2} \\

		\toe_{n-1} & \toe_{n-2}    & \toe_{n-3} & \cdots & \toe_1 & \toe_0 
	\end{pmatrix}
	\Hank = \begin{pmatrix}
		\toe_2 & \toe_3 & \toe_4 & \cdots & \toe_{n-1} & 0 & 0 \\
		\toe_3 & \toe_4 & \toe_5 & \cdots  & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
		\toe_4 & \toe_5 & \toe_6 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \toe_{n-1} \\

	\end{pmatrix}.

\end{equation}
Then define $\ptau := \Toe - H$, where $\Hank$ is a Hankel matrix, that is it is constant on the anti-diagonals. Both $\Toe$ and $\Hank$ are symmetric, so they are completely represented by at most $n$ unique values: the diagonals of $\Toe: \toe_0, \toe_1, \dots, \toe_{n-1}$, and the anti-diagonals of $\Hank: \toe_2, \toe_3, \dots, \toe_{n-1},0,0 $. 
	
The following lemma summarizes properties of $\ptau$.
\begin{lemma}[see \cite{Bini}]
	Let $\Toe$ be a Toeplitz matrix and $\Hank$ the corresponding Hankel correction as in Equation (\ref{Hank}). Then $\ptau = \Toe - \Hank$ has the following properties: 
\begin{enumerate}
%	\item $\ptau$ is a circulant matrix.
	\item $\ptau$ is diagonalized by the type-I discrete sine transform (DST) matrix, $S$. We write $\ptau = S \Lambda S^{-1} = S \Lambda S$.  
	\item $\ptau$ can be applied in $n \log n$ time.
	\item Let $\toe_1$ be the first column of $\Toe$. Then, the $k$-th eigenvalue of $\ptau$ is proportional to the $k$-th entry of $S\toe_1$. Specifically, 
	\begin{equation} \label{eigs} \lambda_k(\ptau) = c_k [S\toe_1]_k, \ \text{where } c_k := \sqrt{\frac{n+1}{2}} \frac{1}{\sin(\frac{\pi k }{n+1})}.\end{equation}
	\item Each eigenvalue of $\ptau$ is written as the truncated function $t_m$ evaluated somewhere on the unit circle, $\lambda_k(\ptau) = \toe_m(z_k)$, where $|z_k| = 1$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}
These preconditioner can also be thought of as coming from the kernel of a displacement operator. This framework is useful for generating yet other circulant preconditioners, (see \cite{Kailath}).

\subsection{AFEM stiffness matrix} \label{stiffness}
Again, let $\A$ be the stiffness matrix resulting from AFEM applied to Equation \ref{FDE}. During the $\texttt{MARK}$ module of AFEM (\ref{AFEM}), it is often the case that adjacent elements have similar error estimates, and are either both marked or both not marked for refinement. As a result, after several refinements there are still subsections of the domain that have been refined to the same level. We refer to these subdomains as \emph{locally uniform}.  Since uniform meshes give rise to SPD Toeplitz systems, if we formed the stiffness matrix for a locally uniform subdomain we would have an SPD Toeplitz matrix. So wherever there are adjacent elements of the same size we find a corresponding Toeplitz block on the main diagonal of our stiffness matrix, $\A$. The size of this block depends on how many adjacent elements are the same size. In practice we have observed that the boundary of the domain almost always requires the greatest level of refinement. In general we have larger Toeplitz blocks from locally uniform subdomains in the middle of the matrix, and smaller blocks---or indeed $1 \times 1$ blocks---near the boundary. We use $\A_d$ to denote the block diagonal with Toeplitz blocks part of $\A$. 

As mentioned previously, the fractional derivative results in a dense matrix, but we observe off-diagonal decay, as elements that are physically separated in the domain have weaker interaction. The rate of decay depends on the fractional order $\alpha$. The low-rank representation makes for fast computations, but complicates both the implementation of the preconditioner and the spectral clustering of the preconditioned system.  Blocks that are most distant from the diagonal contain less information and we assume they have small-norm. We denote these blocks as $\A_F$. Blocks that are adjacent to the diagonal are not in general small-norm, but due to the weaker interaction, are well-suited for low-rank approximation, (see \cite{Xiaozhe} for more details).  These blocks are denoted $\offd$. 
%These assumptions are made precise in section \ref{proof} and numerically validated in section \ref{validate}. 
Using this notation, we write our stiffness matrix as the sum of the Toeplitz blocks, blocks adjacent to the diagonal, and blocks further removed from the diagonal, $\A = \A_D + \offd + \A_F$.

\section{Adaptive \texorpdfstring{$\ptau$}{tau} Preconditioner} \label{precond} 
In this section we set forth the properties we require from a preconditioner, how we use $\ptau$ in building our adaptive $\ptau$ preconditioner, which we call $\Tau$, and how to build and apply $\Tau$ efficiently.

A good preconditioner for an iterative method must in general decrease the total number of iterations, without increasing the cost of a single iteration. We borrow Kailath's \cite{Kailath} criteria for preconditioners, similar criteria is well-established in literature (for example \cite{Bini}). 
\begin{enumerate} 
	\item Complexity of constructing and applying $\Tau \in \R^{m \times m}$ should be $\bigOh (m \log m)$. \label{crit1}
	\item A linear system with $\Tau$ should be solved in $ \bigOh (m \log m)$ operations. \label{crit2}
	\item The spectrum of $\Tau^{-1} \A$ should be clustered around 1. \label{crit3}
\end{enumerate}

How tightly the eigenvalues cluster around 1 determines the speed of convergence. In proving results about the clustering we refer to the infinite matrix framework established previously. First, we summarize results established previously about the spectral clustering of $\ptau$ applied to a single Toeplitz block $\Toe$. This result is then used as a lemma in proving the spectral clustering for our stiffness matrix from the adaptive mesh. 
We make this last point more precise:
\begin{definition}[Eigenvalue Clustering] 
	For any $\varepsilon > 0$ we say the eigenvalues of a matrix $C^{-1} \Toe_m$ are clustered around a real number $\rho$ if there exists $N_1$ and $N_2$ such that for all $m > N_1$ there are at most $N_2$ eigenvalues of $C^{-1} \Toe_m$ that do not lie within $ [\rho-\varepsilon, \rho+\varepsilon]$.
\end{definition}

Assume the stiffness matrix has $k$ Toeplitz blocks $\Toe_1, \Toe_2, \dots, \Toe_k$ of respective sizes $m_1, m_2, \dots, m_k$. Assume additionally these are ordered as they appear along the main diagonal. Each block has a corresponding singly infinite matrix with a corresponding generating function, $\toe_1(z), \toe_2(z), \dots \toe_k(z)$. Assume that for all $1 \leq i \leq k, \toe_i(z)  \sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty} |t_j| <\infty$ and $\toe_i(z) > 0$ for $z$ on the unit circle.

To explicitly construct $\Tau$ for the adaptive system, we calculate the Hankel correction $\Hank_i$ for every Toeplitz block, $\Toe_i$. Then we define $\ptau_i = \Toe_i - \Hank_i$,  resulting in $k$ matrices $\ptau_1, \ptau_2, \dots, \ptau_k$. Finally, we assemble $\Tau$ as the block diagonal matrix with $\ptau_i$ as the $i$-th block. 
$$\Tau = \begin{pmatrix}
		\ptau_1 &&& \\ & \ptau_2 && \\ && \ddots & \\ &&& \ptau_m
	\end{pmatrix}.$$

This explicit construction is not the most efficient, instead we apply $\Tau^{-1}$ implicitly in $m \log m $ time where $m = \sum_{j=1}^k m_j$. Let $S_{m_i}$ denote the type-I DST matrix of size $m_i$.  Using Equation (\ref{eigs}) to calculate the eigenvalues of $\ptau_i$, we write $\ptau_i =  S_{m_i} \Lambda_i S_{m_i}$. 
\begin{equation} \label{diag}
	\Tau^{-1} = 
	\begingroup % keep the change local
	\setlength\arraycolsep{3.5pt}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		S_{m_1} &&& \\ & S_{m_2} && \\ && \ddots & \\ &&& S_{m_k}
	\end{pmatrix}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		\Lambda_1^{-1} &&& \\ & \Lambda_2^{-1} && \\ && \ddots & \\ &&& \Lambda_m^{-1}
	\end{pmatrix}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		S_{m_1} &&& \\ & S_{m_2} && \\ && \ddots & \\ &&& S_{m_k}
		\end{pmatrix}.
	\endgroup
\end{equation}
The DST blocks in the outside matrices are circulant which allows us to use the fast Fourier transform (FFT) to do matrix multiplication in $m \log m $ time. The eigenvalue matrix is diagonal and inverted in $m$ operations, and can be multiplied against a Toeplitz matrix embedded in a $2m \times 2m$ circulant matrix in $ 2m \log 2m$ time. This establishes Criteria (\ref{crit1}) and (\ref{crit2}) for $\Tau$.

In this section we prove that the spectral clustering in criteria (\ref{crit3}) holds for $\Tau$. Proofs for clustering of circulant preconditioned problems in this area tend to follow a similar structure. First it's shown that the preconditioned system having a spectrum clustered around 1 is equivalent to the clustering of a related system around 0. This related system is then split into the sum of a low-rank term and a small-norm term. The small-norm term has spectrum clustered around 0, forcing the clustering of the entire system around 0 with the number of outliers bounded by the rank of the low-rank term.

We present our version of the spectral clustering proof for the case of $\ptau$ acting on a single Toeplitz block. This result is then used to help prove the clustering of the adaptive preconditioner on the adaptive grid, $\Tau^{-1} \A$.

For convenience we state Weyl's inequality and one other lemma that is useful to us later. Both these results are corollaries to the min-max theorem. 
\begin{lemma}[Weyl's Inequality] \\ Let $M$ and $E$ be Hermitian $m \times m$ matrices. Then for $A := M + E$ we have 
	$$ | \lambda_k(A) - \lambda_k(M) | \leq ||E||_2 \ , 1 \leq k \leq m.$$
	That is the eigenvalues of $A$ are at most $||E||_2$ away from the eigenvalues of $M$. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{lemma} \label{eig_lemma}
	Let $T \in \R^{n \times n}$ be an invertible matrix. Let $A \in \R^{n \times n}$, then $$\lambda_k(T^{-1}A) \leq \frac{\lambda_k(A)}{\lambda_{\min}(T)}.$$ 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Using the min-max theorem, 
	\begin{align*}
		\lambda_k ( T^{-1} A) &= \min_{\dim V=k} \max_{x \in V} \left(  \frac{(Ax,x)}{(T x,x)}\right) \\
		&\leq   \min_{\dim V =k} \left[ \max_{x \in V} \left( \frac{(A x,x)}{(x,x)} \right)  \max_{x \in V} \left( \frac{(x,x)}{(T x,x)} \right) \right] \\
		&\leq \left[  \min_{\dim V =k}  \max_{x \in V} \left( \frac{(A x,x)}{(x,x)} \right)  \right] \max_{x \in \R^n} \left( \frac{(x,x)}{(T x,x)} \right)  \\
		&= \lambda_k(A)   \max_{x \in \R^n} \left( \frac{(x,x)}{(T x,x)} \right) \leq \lambda_k(A)  \frac{1}{\lambda_{\min}(T)}. 
	\end{align*}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Spectral Clustering of a Toeplitz Block}
\begin{lemma}[Eigenvalue Clustering on a Toeplitz Block] \label{block}
	Let $\Toe$ be a singly infinite, symmetric, Toeplitz matrix with diagonals $\toe_0, \toe_1, \dots$. Then $\Toe_m$ is its $m \times m$ truncation with diagonals $\toe_0, \toe_1, \dots, \toe_{m-1}$. Assume the generating function $\toe(z)$ is in the Wiener class and positive on the unit circle. Let $\ptau$ be the corresponding preconditioner, then for $m$ large enough the spectrum of $\ptau^{-1} \Toe$ is clustered around 1. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Since $\toe(z) > 0$ for $|z|=1$, compactness of the unit circle implies that for some $\varepsilon > 0$, $\toe(z) > 2 \varepsilon$ when $|z|= 1$. By the Wiener class assumption we choose $N$ such that $\sum_{j=N}^{\infty} \toe_j z^j \leq \sum_{j=N}^{\infty} |\toe_j| < \varepsilon$. So for all $m \geq N$, 
	$$ 2 \varepsilon < \toe(z) = \toe_m(z) + \sum_{j=m}^{\infty} \toe_j z^j < \toe_m(z) + \varepsilon.$$
	Thus $\toe_m(z) > \varepsilon$ on the unit circle. 

Recall that by construction $\ptau= \Toe - \Hank$, where $\Hank$ is the Hankel matrix with anti-diagonals $\toe_2, \toe_3, \dots, \toe_{m-1},0,0$. So  $\Toe = \ptau + \Hank$ and $\ptau^{-1} \Toe = I + \ptau^{-1} \Hank $. Therefore it suffices to show that the eigenvalues of $\ptau^{-1} \Hank$ are clustered around 0. 

We split $\Hank$ into a low-rank matrix $\Hank_{LR}$ that contains the anti-diagonals $\toe_0, \dots, \toe_N$ and a small-norm matrix $\Hank_{SN}$ such that $\Hank = \Hank_{LR} + \Hank_{SN}$.  Let $s := \text{rank}(\Hank_{LR}) << m$. The small-norm descriptor is justified since $\Hank_{SN} = \Hank - \Hank_{LR}$ is a hermitian $m \times m$ matrix with at most two copies of $\toe_{N}, \dots, \toe_{m-1}$ in each row/column. Thus $||\Hank_{SN}||_2 = \sqrt{||\Hank_{SN}||_1 ||\Hank_{SN}||_{\infty}} = ||\Hank_{SN}||_1 < 2 \varepsilon$. Hence by Weyl's Inequality at least $m-s$ of the eigenvalues of $\Hank_{SN}$ are clustered within $2 \varepsilon$ of zero. 

Now by Lemma (\ref{eig_lemma}),
\begin{align*}
	\lambda_k ( \ptau^{-1} \Hank  ) &\leq \lambda_k (\Hank)  \frac{1}{\lambda_{\min}(\ptau )} \\
	 & = \lambda_k (\Hank)  \frac{1}{a_m(z_{\min})} \leq \lambda_k (\Hank)  \frac{1}{\varepsilon}.
\end{align*}
Thus, the clustering of the spectrum of $\Hank$ around 0 implies the same for $\ptau^{-1} \Hank$. 
\end{proof}

\subsection{Spectral Clustering of the Full System} \label{proof}
\begin{theorem}[Full Matrix Clustering]
	Let $\A$ be the stiffness matrix as described in Section (\ref{stiffness}) and $\Tau$ as described in Section (\ref{precond}). Then the spectrum of $\Tau^{-1} \A$ is clustered around 1. 
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
% First notice that $\Tau^{-1} \Hank$ is block diagonal and the spectrum of each block can be characterized using the former proof on each block. 

Recall that the stiffness matrix is dense with Toeplitz blocks on the diagonal (denoted $\A_D$), low-rank blocks adjacent to the diagonal (denoted $\offd$, and small-norm blocks elsewhere (denoted $\A_F$), so that $\A = \A_D + \offd + \A_F$. Let $\Hank$ be the block diagonal Hankel Correction matrix, observing then that $ \A_D = \Tau + \Hank$ we see that $\A = \Tau + \Hank + \offd + \A_F$. Our approach is to split $\Hank + \offd + \A_F$ into a small-norm term $A_{SN}$ and a low-rank term $\A_{LR}$. Thus 
\begin{equation}
		\Tau^{-1} \A = \Tau^{-1}(\Tau + \A_{SN} + \A_{LR} ) = I + \Tau^{-1}\A_{SN}  + \Tau^{-1}\A_{LR}.
\end{equation}
Hence, it suffices to show that the spectrum of $\Tau^{-1}\A_{SN}$ is clustered around 0 and that $\Tau^{-1}\A_{LR}$ contributes a bounded number of eigenvalue outliers. 

Recall that blocks in $\offd$ are well-approximated as a low-rank matrix. To that end, we begin by examining a single block in $\offd$, call it $\off$. Let $\off$ be a $m_{\off} \times m_{\off}$ matrix with approximation rank $r_{\off}$. Then we split $\off$ via its singular value decomposition (SVD):  
$$ \off = \left( \sum_{i=1}^{r_{\off}} \sigma_i^{(\off)} \u_i ^{(\off)}\v_i^{(\off)}* \right) + \left(  \sum_{i=r_{\off}+1}^{m_{\off}} \sigma_i^{(\off)} \u_i^{(\off)} \v_i^{(\off)}*  \right). $$ 
In the above equation, $\u$ and $\v$ are length $m_{\off}$ vectors. With a slight abuse of notation, we embed these in length $m$ vectors to express the block matrix $\offd$ as the SVD of its blocks. Let $\setoff$ represent the set of off-diagonal blocks in $\offd$. Define $r := \max_{\off \in \setoff} r_{\off}$ and $M := \max_{\off \in \setoff} m_{\off}$. We now express $\offd$ as the sum of the SVD of its blocks, and then regroup terms into a low-rank sum and a small-norm sum. 
\begin{align*}
	\offd &= \sum_{\off \in \setoff} \left[ \left( \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i^{(\off)} \u_i ^{(\off)}\v_i^{(\off)}* \right) + \left(  \sum_{i=r+1}^{m_{\off}} \sigma_i^{(\off)} \u_i^{(\off)} \v_i^{(\off)}*  \right) \right]  \\
	&= \left( \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i* \right) + \left(  \sum_{i=r+1}^{M} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i*  \right) .
\end{align*}

As before, we have $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N$ much smaller than $m$ so that for every Toeplitz block the truncated function for the block $a_{m_i} (z) > \varepsilon$ on the unit circle and $\sum_{i = N}^{\infty} |a_i| < \varepsilon$. 
We additionally split $\Hank$ by separating into $\Hank_{LR}$ the anti-diagonals with coefficients $a_0, \dots, a_N$ and into $\Hank_{SN}$ the anti-diagonals comprising of $a_{N+1}, \dots a_{m_i}$ for each block. So we have two splittings, 
\begin{align*} 
	\offd &= \left( \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i* \right) + \left(  \sum_{i=r+1}^{M} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i*  \right), \\
	\Hank &= \Hank_{SN} + \Hank_{LR}.
\end{align*}
The first term in each sum can be thought of as the low-rank equivalent from before and similarly the second term is the small-norm summand.
Finally, we make the splitting $\A = \Tau + \A_{SN} + \A_{LR}$, where
\begin{align*}
	\A_{SN} &= \Hank_{SN}+ \sum_{i=r+1}^{M} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i* +A_F, \\
	\A_{LR} &= \Hank_{LR} + \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i*.
\end{align*}
% \todo[inline]{Bound on number of off diagonal blocks} 
Now $s := \text{rank}(\Tau^{-1}\A_{LR}) \leq \text{rank}(\A_{LR}) \leq 2(N-1) + r$ bounds the number of outlier eigenvalues. It remains to show $|| \Tau^{-1}\A_{SN}||_2 \leq \varepsilon$. Define $\tilde{\offd} = \sum_{i=r_{\offd}+1}^{n_{\offd}} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i* $, so that $\A_{SN} = \Hank_{SN} + \tilde{\offd} + \A_F$. 
\begin{align*}
	|| \Tau^{-1}\A_{SN}||_2 &\leq ||\Tau^{-1} \Hank_{SN}||_2 + ||\Tau^{-1} \tilde{\offd}||_2 +||\Tau^{-1} \A_F ||_2
\end{align*}
Since the block multiplication $\Tau^{-1} \Hank_{SN}$ is each $\ptau$ acting on its corresponding Hankel correction we bound it by Lemma (\ref{block}). 
We bound $||\Tau^{-1} \A_F||_2$ with Weyl's inequality:
\begin{equation*}
	||\Tau^{-1} \A_F||_2 \leq ||\Tau^{-1}||_2 ||\A_F||_2 =\sigma_{\max}(\Tau^{-1}) \sigma_{\max}(\A_F) = \frac{ \sigma_{\max}(\A_F)}{\lambda_{\min}(\Tau)}.
\end{equation*}
Since $\A_F$ are small-norm this value is small. 
Finally, we bound $||\Tau^{-1} \tilde{\offd}||_2$. Using lemma (\ref{eig_lemma}) 
\begin{align*}
	\lambda_k ( \Tau^{-1} \tilde{\offd}) &\leq \lambda_k(\tilde{\offd})  \frac{1}{\lambda_{\min}(\Tau)} \\
	&= \lambda_k(\tilde{\offd})  \min_{n \in n_k} \min_{1 \leq i \leq n} \frac{\sin(\frac{\pi i}{n+1})}{\sum_{j=1}^n t_j \sin(\frac{\pi i j }{n+1})}. 
\end{align*}
% \todo[inline]{Where to stop with $\lambda_{\min}\Tau$? can take it to generating function or bound on gen func or expression for eigs (like above) or leave as is} 
To finish the proof we utilize the fact that the matrices are SPD and relate the $k$-th eigenvalue of the preconditioned matrices to their 2-norm, 
\begin{align*}
	|| \Tau^{-1}\A_{SN}||_2 &\leq ||\Tau^{-1} \Hank_{SN}||_2 + ||\Tau^{-1} A||_2 +||\Tau^{-1} \A_F ||_2 \\
	&= \lambda_{\max}(\Tau^{-1} \Hank_{SN}) + \lambda_{\max}(\Tau^{-1} \tilde{\offd}) + \lambda_{\max}(\Tau^{-1} \A_F) \\
	&\leq \frac{1}{\lambda_{\min}(\Tau)} \left( \lambda_{\max}(\Hank_{SN}) +  \lambda_{\max}(\tilde{\offd}) + \lambda_{\max}(\A_F) \right). 
\end{align*}
%Since $\tilde{\offd}$ made of blocks that have form $\sum_{i=r_{\offd}+1}^{n_{\offd}} \sigma_i \u_i \v_i*$ what can we say about $\lambda_k$?  
\end{proof}

% \todo[inline]{Possible comments to add: \\ - technically lots of $ 1 \times 1$ blocks at boundaries, these get jacobi? inverse so are clustered around 1 \\ - all problems come from boundaries \\ - proof easily extended to different kinds of circulant preconditioner}
In practice the block size, $m_i$, is determined by the AFEM algorithm, which in turn dictates the actual size $\varepsilon$ and hence the strength of clustering. So bigger Toeplitz blocks in $\A$ result in tighter eigenvalue clustering. 

Notice that extending the proof from one Toeplitz block to the adaptive stiffness matrix relies only on being able to split the block into a small-norm and a low-rank piece. Thus, nothing about this proof is overly-specific to $\ptau$ and the methods could easily be extended to the other types of circulant preconditioners in \cite{Chan_89, Chan_Strang, Chan_Yeung}.

\section{Conclusion}
We have shown how to build a preconditioner for FDEs discretized by AFEM. The spectrum of the preconditioned system is clustered around 1 and accelerates convergence of iterative methods. In the future, we will investigate how to customize the AFEM \texttt{MARK} module to build bigger Toeplitz blocks and thus get tighter clusters. Finally, we will investigate how to use these techniques to build preconditioners for FDEs discretized with AFEM on higher dimensional meshes.
